As designers and humans we love white space.
Great swathes of it as far as the eye can see!
Well maybe not THAT far but, quite far. It not only gives us some breathing room between elements, but it helps give the ole eyeballs somewhere to rest. It's calming. Take these two images for example - which do you think looks most "restful"?

Now unless you're a statistical anomoly (in which case.. erm. sorry?) you're going to prefer the image on the right. The space between the things make it look more calm and clear. It has the added bonus of being able to showcase the indiviudal items more.
Now try this for me, take a look at the picture on the left, and then close your eyes. Can you tell me how many pens and penils there are in the image?


No?


Of course you can't.
Now, look at the picture on the right and then close your eyes. Can you remember how many pens there are? One. You're right! See how much easier it was to identify the individual items? That's because of the white space between the items and the white space around the image as a whole

So, now I've (hopefully) demonstrated how important white space is for design in general, how does this relate to coding? And what on earth does it have to do with margins, borders and padding (oh my!) - sorry, can't resist..
Well these three CSS styling tools are going to be invaluable in helping us create that big ole sea of white space that we all crave. Let's start at the outside and work our way down - like an onion.

The Outer Layers

So if I use the homepage of my website as an example, we can see that the image should go right to the edge of the screen like this because I have my margin set to 0px.

Now if I wanted to add a margin, it would end up looking something more like this;

Do you see the space we've added all around the website? That's the margin - the space around the outside of your content.
This works will all element blocks, like divs, images, paragraphs etc, you can add a margin (the space around the outside) to all of them.
We add the margin by using the css syntax of [ margin: --px ] (or whatever measurement you're using.) There are of course many ways to edit the margin,
but I'll save that for another blog post - for now, this will do.


Down a layer we go.

The Border Layer

Now we come to the border.
While technically this is a way of adding "space" to an element, it's often used more like a decorative addition. It does exactly what it says on the tin
- adds a border. If we go back to our website example, right now we've added the margin (the space around the outside of the element - good well remembered!) so now let's add a border.
I'm going to add a lovely solid pink chunky border to the whole of the website. What do you think?

Now aethetically, this is kinda trash. But you get the idea of how the border works?
Again, there are many ways to add a border and mess around with how it looks,
but the syntax is probably how you'd expect it to be [ border: --px ] with a [ border-style: solid ] and [ border-color: #FAB8E5 ]

The Padding Layer [the final boss]

Okay, so we have padding around the outside of our website called the....? Margin! Yes!
And then we have this aesthetically pleasing what around the edge of our website? Border! Yeah!
Now we think okay, well I like the spacing, but I feel like all the "stuff" in my website is too close to the edge. How do we fix that? With padding!
A good way to think of padding, is like wrapping your content in bubblewrap, to protect it from the "edges" of your container (be that a div, or box or ehat have you)
