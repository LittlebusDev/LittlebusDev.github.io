Where to start?

At this point in our journey, we've gone over HTML (the main heart of our website) CSS (the "pretty" layer of our website) and we have a pretty okay (albeit static) website. Now, how do we interact with it? JavaScript! And an understanding of the DOM.

So what is JavaScript?

Javascript is another coding language (like HTML and CSS) and this is how we do things like make working buttons, make things sparkle, make things move, add comments, like, share, purchase etc. Think of it as the final layer of a website to take it from a photograph to a moving interactable video.

Do you have a handy-dandy analogy for me?

Yeah! I do! So a slightly easier way to think of JavaScript in relation to HTML and CSS would be like a lock and a key. In this example, lets say that the lock is the HTML and the key is the CSS. A lock is a useful item, it's fine just as it is. It's a lock. Cool. We love locks. JavaScript in this analogy would be the ACT of using the key to unlock and lock the lock. So without Javascript, the lock and key would just be a lock and key. But WITH JavaScript, it's a functioning lockable and unlockable thing.

Does that make sense?

So how do we interact?

Woah woah, hang on. Before we interact, we need to learn about how the information is read by our browsers.

Let's think of our HTML being read like making a mug with clay. There are a few steps to get us from wet clay to solid mug, and they all have to be done in a particular order, otherwise it won't work.

(1) So we need to start with wedging the clay and getting it ready (otherwise our mug could crack in the kiln)

(2) Then we need to smack the clay around a little bit (otherwise the mug could warp in the kiln)

(3) Then we throw the clay on a wheel (otherwise our mug would just be a lump of clay)

(4) Then we need to leave it to dry so we can trim it (otherwise our mug would be ugly)

Then we trim and add a handle (otherwise our mug would be a pot)

(5) Then we leave it to dry all the way to bone dry (otherwise our mug could explode in the kiln)

(6) Then we bisque fire our mug (otherwise our mug won't be waterproof)

(7) Then we glaze our mug (otherwise our mug won't be safe to drink out of)

(8) Then we glaze fire our mug (otherwise the glaze would just be a toxic powdery mess)



AND NOW WE HAVE A MUG!


So these steps need to be carried out in a specific order, to get us to our mug. There's a very specific order, otherwise it won't work. This is known as "CONTROL FLOW" and that is what our JavaScript is creating for the DOM. The JavaScript is the way we tell the DOM what order to read our code.

So we've given our DOM a way to read our HTML & CSS (mug making instructions) and the DOM will read it from top to bottom and only progress if the statment has been completed (e.g the clay has been wedged, so now we go to smacking it a bit, once that's done we're move onto throwing and so on)

But what if we wanted to change our order? What if we wanted to underglaze so we glaze fire and bisque fire at the same time? Or what if we wanted to add another step? We can do that with something called "CONDITIONALS" and "LOOPS"

What's a conditional?

In coding, they're if..else statements. "If this happens, do this, or if it doesn't happen, do that." If we bring back our mug making example, it would be "if you want to underglaze, make this your working order -

(1), (2), (3), (4), (5), (7), (6).

Otherwise make your working order

(1), (2), (3), (4), (5), (6), (7), (8).


Still with me?

Okay, so what's a loop?

Looping is asking the code to repeat steps, over and over again until your conditions have been met (or not if you forget to put one)

So in our example, let's say we have a relatively large kiln, and we don't want to waste time and money by only firing one item at a time. We would make a few more mugs, to fill the kiln and fire them all toegther right? So we would "LOOP" our process -
start at (1), and progress all the way to (5), [that's one loop]
until we have enough mugs to fill the kiln, then stop.

That my friend is a "LOOP". Keep doing this one thing, until this thing happens. You need to have an end point for your "LOOP" otherwise this would keep happening over and over and over and over again until you run out of studio space, or your machine crashes.
